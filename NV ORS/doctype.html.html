                    <h3 class="text-2xl font-semibold mb-4 text-[#2C3E50]">Pharmacist's Role & Physiology</h3>

                    <p class="mb-4">Nausea and vomiting are common, distressing symptoms encountered daily in community pharmacy. As pharmacists, our role is essential in assessing symptoms, identifying potential causes, recommending appropriate therapies, and providing crucial patient counseling to ensure safe and effective management and prevent complications like dehydration.</p>

                    <p class="mb-4">The vomiting reflex is a complex process coordinated by the <span class="font-semibold text-[#D35400]">Vomiting Center</span> in the medulla. It integrates signals from four major pathways: the Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ), the Vestibular System, the GI Tract, and the Cerebral Cortex. Understanding these pathways and their primary neurotransmitters is key to selecting the appropriate antiemetic.</p>

                        <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">H1 Antihistamines & Anticholinergics</h3>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600"><strong>Mechanism:</strong> Block H1 and/or muscarinic receptors in the vomiting center and vestibular system. Ideal for motion-related nausea.</p>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600"><strong>Key Counseling:</strong> Advise caution with driving due to significant <span class="font-semibold">sedation</span>. Watch for anticholinergic effects like dry mouth and confusion, especially in the elderly.</p>

                        <div class="space-y-4">

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Meclozine + Pyridoxine (B6):</strong> Primarily for NVP and motion sickness. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Navidoxine, Vominore (1.75 JD)</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Promethazine:</strong> For N/V and motion sickness. High sedation. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Histazin (0.69-1 JD), Promet (0.57 JD)</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Cinnarizine:</strong> For vestibular disorders (Meniere's disease) and motion sickness. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Stugeron (1.90 JD), Vertizin (1.10-1.70 JD)</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Dimenhydrinate / Diphenhydramine:</strong> Classic options for motion sickness and NVP.</div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Scopolamine Patch:</strong> Prophylaxis for motion sickness. Counsel on proper application.</div>

                        </div>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div class="tab-pane hidden" id="d2">

                     <div class="bg-white p-6 rounded-lg shadow-md">

                        <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">Dopamine ($D_2$) Receptor Antagonists</h3>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600"><strong>Mechanism:</strong> Block $D_2$ receptors in the CTZ. Some, like Metoclopramide, also have prokinetic effects, enhancing gastric emptying.</p>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600"><strong>Key Counseling:</strong> Watch for extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) like tremors or muscle spasms. Contraindicated in Parkinson's disease.</p>

                        <div class="space-y-4">

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Metoclopramide:</strong> For gastroparesis, migraines, PONV. Higher risk of CNS side effects. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Clopram (0.75-1.09 JD), Metoclop, Primperan</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Domperidone:</strong> Less CNS penetration, so lower risk of EPS. Can cause QT prolongation. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Motilium (2.67 JD), Domperide (1.86-2.81 JD), Costi (2.34 JD)</span></div>

                        </div>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div class="tab-pane hidden" id="ht3">

                     <div class="bg-white p-6 rounded-lg shadow-md">

                        <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">Serotonin (5-HT3) Receptor Antagonists</h3>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600"><strong>Mechanism:</strong> Potent blockade of 5-HT3 receptors in the CTZ and GI tract. The cornerstone for chemotherapy-induced N/V (CINV) and PONV.</p>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600"><strong>Key Counseling:</strong> Generally well-tolerated with less sedation. Main side effect is constipation. Monitor for QT prolongation, especially with other QT-prolonging drugs.</p>

                        <div class="space-y-4">

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Ondansetron:</strong> Highly effective for CINV, PONV, and severe, refractory NVP. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Zofran, Onda (3.65-19.82 JD)</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Others (Granisetron, Palonosetron):</strong> Primarily used in hospital/oncology settings for CINV.</div>

                        </div>

                    </div>

                </div>

                     <div class="bg-white p-6 rounded-lg shadow-md">

                        <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">Other Antiemetic Agents</h3>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600">This category includes a diverse group of agents used for specific or refractory situations, often in combination with other antiemetics.</p>

                        <div class="space-y-4">

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Ginger:</strong> Natural OTC option for mild nausea, especially in NVP.</div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Glucocorticoids (e.g., Dexamethasone):</strong> Effective for CINV and refractory N/V. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Dexamed Amp (12.43 JD)</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>NK1 Receptor Antagonists (e.g., Aprepitant):</strong> Primarily for preventing delayed CINV, used in combination.</div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Benzodiazepines (e.g., Lorazepam):</strong> Weak antiemetics, used for anxiety-related (anticipatory) nausea.</div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Betahistine:</strong> Specifically for Meniere's disease-related vertigo and nausea. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Betaserc (7.22-10.26 JD), Anatax (3.64-6.38 JD)</span></div>

                        </div>

                    </div>

                </div>

                <div class="tab-pane hidden" id="adjunctive">

                     <div class="bg-white p-6 rounded-lg shadow-md">

                        <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-2">Adjunctive Therapy (Acid-Reducing Agents)</h3>

                        <p class="mb-4 text-gray-600">Not primary antiemetics, but useful when N/V is associated with heartburn or acid reflux. Pharmacists can often recommend these OTC.</p>

                        <div class="space-y-4">

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>Antacids:</strong> Fast-acting, short duration. Neutralize stomach acid. Counsel on separating doses from other meds (e.g., iron, antibiotics). <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Examples: Gaviscon, Maalox, Rennie</span></div>

                             <div class="p-4 border rounded-lg"><strong>H2 Blockers (e.g., Famotidine):</strong> Slower onset, longer duration. Reduce acid production. Famotidine is often preferred over Cimetidine due to fewer drug interactions. <span class="text-sm font-mono bg-gray-100 p-1 rounded">Jordan Brands: Famodar, Peptifam</span></div>

                        </div>

        <section id="ors" class="mb-16 bg-white p-8 rounded-lg shadow-lg">

            <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold text-center mb-6 text-[#2C3E50]">Oral Rehydration Solutions (ORS)</h2>

            <div class="text-center max-w-3xl mx-auto">

                <p class="text-lg mb-4">The cornerstone of managing fluid loss from vomiting and diarrhea is ORS. Its specific composition of water, electrolytes, and glucose is designed to match what the body needs, using the glucose-sodium co-transport mechanism for rapid and effective absorption.</p>

                <p class="font-semibold text-xl text-[#D35400] mb-6">It is the first-line treatment for mild to moderate dehydration from any cause.</p>

                <div class="grid md:grid-cols-2 gap-6 text-left">

                    <div class="bg-orange-50 p-4 rounded-lg">

                        <h4 class="font-bold text-lg mb-2">Key Counseling Points</h4>

                        <ul class="list-disc list-inside space-y-2">

                            <li>Mix powder sachet with the correct amount of clean water as per package instructions.</li>

                            <li>Advise taking <span class="font-semibold">small, frequent sips</span> to avoid triggering more vomiting.</li>

                            <li>Refrigerate prepared solution and discard after 24 hours.</li>

                            <li>Contraindicated in renal failure.</li>

                        </ul>

                    </div>

                     <div class="bg-blue-50 p-4 rounded-lg">
